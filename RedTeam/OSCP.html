Why is OSCP Still One of the Most Respected Certs in Cybersecurity

Who is OffSec?

OffSec, short for Offensive Security, is the company behind some of the most hardcore, hands-on security training in the industry. They’re the creators of Kali Linux and Metasploit FW, the popular penetration testing distribution used worldwide. OffSec isn’t just a training provider, they’re a mindset. Their motto, “Try Harder,” is infamous in the hacking community and sets the tone for everything they do. Their courses aren’t designed to spoon-feed you answers; they’re built to challenge you, push your limits, and turn you into a real-world problem solver.

Site: Offsec


What is OSCP (PEN-200) ?

If you’ve been exploring the world of ethical hacking and penetration testing, you’ve probably come across the acronym OSCP. It's more than just a certification, it's a rite of passage for many aspiring offensive security professionals. But what exactly is OSCP? Who offers it, and what makes it so special? Let’s break it down. OSCP stands for Offensive Security Certified Professional. It’s the flagship certification tied to the PEN-200 course, which focuses on penetration testing with Kali Linux. PEN-200 is a deep dive into the core skills every ethical hacker needs: reconnaissance, vulnerability identification, exploitation, privilege escalation, and post-exploitation, all bundled into a self-paced, hands-on curriculum. Think of it as a structured path to becoming a professional hacker. But don’t expect flashy videos or long lectures, you'll get lab machines to attack, a course manual, and a clear goal "Learn by doing". The content is dense, the labs are tough, and the learning curve is real, but that’s exactly what makes it so valuable.

Site: PEN-200


Who Is It For?

OSCP is not an entry-level cert, and you don’t need to be a senior pentester to take it, you should have a solid understanding of basic networking, Linux, Windows, and scripting. If you’ve got some experience in cybersecurity, are comfortable on the command line, and you enjoy solving problems by breaking things, OSCP is a good fit.

It’s especially ideal for:
  • Aspiring penetration testers
  • Red teamers in training
  • Security engineers who want to move into offensive roles
  • Anyone looking to prove they can hack it, literally and figuratively

What Will You Learn?

The PEN-200 course is broken into modules that cover real-world topics like:
  • Enumeration and scanning techniques
  • Exploiting buffer overflows
  • Tunneling, pivoting, and Active Directory attacks
  • Custom exploit development (basic level)
  • Post-exploitation tactics

You’re expected to think like an attacker. The course is loaded with vulnerable lab machines that simulate real-world networks, and you’re given minimal guidance "Black Box". You have to investigate, try, fail, research, and try again. That’s the OffSec way. By the time you're done, you'll be more than just technically proficient, you'll have the confidence to approach unknown systems with a hacker’s mindset.

What’s the Exam Like?

The OSCP exam is legendary, you get 23 hours and 45 minutes to hack into a set of machines and gain administrative access (or "root" them). The exam environment includes multiple standalone machines and often an Active Directory lab. You need at least 70 out of 100 points to pass, and points are earned by compromising systems and submitting a detailed penetration test report afterward. This isn’t a multiple-choice test. This is you, alone in a lab, facing real machines with real vulnerabilities. You’ll be exhausted, frustrated, and possibly questioning your life choices around hour 16. But if you push through and pass, you’ll have something no textbook can give you: proof that you can perform under pressure in a simulated real-world attack scenario.


What mindset shifts are necessary to succeed in OSCP compared to traditional certifications?

The biggest shift? You have to unlearn the “study, memorize, and regurgitate” method. OSCP doesn’t care how many acronyms you know or how well you perform on flashcards. You’re tossed into real-world scenarios where you have to think like an attacker, and that means embracing failure "A lot of it". You’ll hit dead ends, tools will fail, xxploits won’t work,and there’s no "Hint" to help you. So the mindset shift is this: stop looking for the shortcut, start enjoying the process of researching, experimenting, and troubleshooting. Google becomes your best friend, Documentation becomes your roadmap. The exam, and even the labs, force you to build grit and persistence. Remember, it’s not about speed, it’s about patience, curiosity, and resilience. Moreover, In traditional certs, the goal is to pass, but in OSCP, the goal is to become capable and that’s a major mental shift.


What are some effective strategies or tools that helped candidates survive the 24-hour exam window?

The exam isn’t just a test of skill, it’s a test of strategy, time management, and mental endurance. Here are some battle-tested strategies many successful OSCPs swear by:

  1. Enumeration First, Always: Don’t rush to exploit, and spend quality time enumerating each machine. Most of the time, the path forward is sitting right in front of you but only if you’re thorough. Watch out of rabbit holes
  2. Keep Notes While Hacking: Use something like MaindMap, CherryTree, Obsidian, or even simple Markdown files to track IPs, open ports, credentials, exploit steps, and failed attempts. You’ll save precious time revisiting them later.
  3. Timeboxing: Don’t burn 8 hours on one machine. Give yourself 2–3 hours max and if you’re stuck, move on and come back later with fresh eyes. This alone saves a ton of mental fatigue. 3h Max on each machine
  4. Automate Where Possible: Tools like AutoRecon, nmap scripts, linPEAS, winPEAS, and enum4linux can cut down redundant effort. Have them ready to go. Make sure not to use black listed tools
  5. Stay Healthy: Hydrate, snack smart, and take breaks. This sounds cheesy, but it makes a huge difference. Sitting for 24 hours without moving will kill your focus. Plan a 20-minute nap or short walk around every 1h, it resets your brain.
  6. Practice Your Reporting Early: Many people forget that the exam includes a full pentest report. Writing clean, professional documentation while tired is rough. Get a template ready and practice report writing during lab work.

What are the pros and cons of the "Try Harder" philosophy

The "Try Harder" motto from OffSec is iconic, it’s a hacker-style. It encapsulates the idea that success in cybersecurity doesn’t come from being handed answers but from digging deep, solving problems yourself, and building mental toughness. That said, in today’s fast-moving infosec landscape, this mindset has both strengths and serious limitations.


Pros of “Try Harder”:-

  1. Builds Deep Problem-Solving Skills: You're not memorizing commands, you’re learning how to think. This is huge in real-world pentesting where no two environments are alike. "Try Harder" forces you to research, experiment, and adapt and that what makes OSCP grads so respected.
  2. Fosters Independence: In a field where you’ll often be the only red teamer in a blue-heavy team, or where you’re reverse-engineering malware without much documentation, self-reliance is gold. The philosophy trains you to solve problems with minimal support.
  3. Creates Mental Toughness: OSCP isn't just technical, it’s also psychological. You’ll fail repeatedly, but when you finally get that root shell or escalate that privilege, you realize you didn’t just learn a skill but "You earned it".
  4. Mirrors Real-World Challenges: There’s no walkthrough in real attacks, so you have to dig through logs, pivot through obscure services, or brute-force an odd protocol. OSCP’s brutal learning curve prepares you for those scenarios better than most certs.

Cons of “Try Harder”:-

  1. It Can Alienate Beginners: While the philosophy aims to build resilience, it often feels like a gatekeeping tool. Newcomers without strong foundational skills may feel lost, overwhelmed, or worse "discouraged". Not everyone thrives under pressure and ambiguity.
  2. Inefficient in a High-Speed Industry: In real-world work, sometimes “trying harder” isn't the best use of time. You ask a senior, you Google smarter, you automate. Spending five hours stuck on a problem that a 5 minute team discussion could solve isn’t efficient, it’s reckless.
  3. No Built-In Collaboration: Security is shifting toward purple teaming, shared knowledge, and open-source collaboration. "Try Harder" sometimes runs counter to that culture by promoting isolation over teamwork. That can create silos, even unintentionally.
  4. It Doesn’t Scale for Everyone: Not every learner is wired the same way. Some need structure, mentorship, or community. Solo approach might create rockstars, but it can also burn people out or create imposter syndrome when they hit walls.

Final Thought

"Try Harder" still has a place in infosec, but it shouldn’t be the only guiding light. A modern take might be “Try Smarter, Ask Sharper, Push Deeper.” The core value should remain but we also need to pair it with smarter tooling, better collaboration, and inclusive learning strategies. Because in coming years, it’s not just about hacking alone in a dark room, it’s about hacking together, in a world that moves fast and breaks everything.


Most importantly, trust your prep. The exam isn't about being perfect, it's about Try Hard and Be Smart.
